# How did LVMH became the world's leading luxury group 

## Company Overview

### Executive Summary
LVMH has built the world's largest luxury conglomerate not by standardizing brands, but by preserving the identity of each Maison while leveraging the scale of a global group. LVMH was created in 1987 when Moët Hennessy and Louis Vuitton decided to unify, and Bernaurd Arnault has led the LVMH group since 1989, being the majority shareholder. This unification opened a new pathway for the luxury industry, and currently LVMH is the world leader in luxury having brands in all six luxury sectors. The main mission of LVMH is defined as "the art of crafting dreams", and all products and experiences focus on delivering excellence and meeting the highest ethical, social, and environmental standards. LVMH masterfully is involved in the fevelopment and growth of their maisons, while at the same time ensuring they keep their identity, heritage and unique branding. This is what makes LVMH unqiue, they are not just a simple comoany which owns a few maisons, they have more than 75 maisons across all six luxury sectors, and through the years have masterfully helped their growth and development while procuring their unique essence. In this essay* you will find a complete analysis of LVMH, focusing on its history, business model, financial analysis and what is it that makes LVMH such a powerhouse in the luxury industry

### Company History
LVMH was created in 1987 with the merge of Louis Vuitton and Möet Hennessy, but this brands history goes further back in time. Louis Vuitton is one of the most recognized fashion luxury brands worldwide, and it goes back to 1837 to a 16 year-old boy named Louis Vuitton arrived to Paris by foot and became a valued craftsman, specialized in travel boxes and trunks at the Parisian atelier of Monsieur Maréchal, which 17 years later would allowed him to open his own workshop. His work was renowned not only for his impeccable craftsmanship and designs, but for an unpickable lock which protected travellers from burglars. On the other hand, Möet Hennessy was also a result from a merger from Möet & Chandon and Hennessy. Möet & Chandon, initially started as Möet and et Cie, created by wine trader Claude Möet in 1743, where he specialized on transporting champagne to Paris, coinciding with an increase of demand for sparkling wine by the court of King Louis XV.  Around the same time, an Irish Jacobite serving under King Louis XV army decided to retire to the cognac region of France and establish a brandy trading house called Hennessy. Richard Hennesy set his distillery apart by capitalizing on early international trade while delivering exquisite Frenchmen craftmanship. In 1794 it sends its first shipments to America, a recently independent country, where his popularity rapidly increased and deep-rooted his influence in the American people, particularly in the African American community and culture. Almost two centuries later, the two historical brands decided to merge in 1971 creating the wine and spirit giant Möet Hennessy, and just 16 years later, they finalized the merge with Louis Vuitton, giving way to the world’s leading company in luxury, LVMH. At this time, there were concerns regarding corporate hostile takeovers, as the luxury market was becoming more international, economies started to grow, there was a higher need for large investment quantities and costs, and new diversified companies where rising, which specialized in more than just one luxury sector, causing Louis Vuitton and Möet Hennessy to seek a merge in a form of protection against the growth of the luxury sector. The merge was finalized in 1987, but there where immediately problems, as the co-founders of LVMH Henry Racamier (Louis Vuitton) and Alain Chevalier (Möet Hennessy), where not seeing eye to eye, which allowed Bernard Arnault to stage a successful hostile takeover of the company by gradually purchasing shares of the company until he became majority shareholder, chairman and CEO, taking control of LVMH and evicting the two original founders in 1989.

### Leadership

LVMH follows a decentralized organization model, which main purpose is to keep each Maison autonomous and reactive. They also have a vertical integration which allows LVMH to provide the absolute best quality of products and ensure all aspects of the production are up to regulations and standards set by the groups. Since 1989 Bernard Arnault has been CEO and Chairman of the LVMH group, and he is both part of the executive committee and the board of directors. The executive committee is formed by 14 members, diverse in gender, who head the Group's operational and functional entities. Their main duty is to assess strategies and put into motion goals and objectives set by the board of directors. The board of directors is composed by 77% of French executives yet has a just gender parity. The board focuses on formulating LVMH’s strategy to ensure growth while protecting the group’s interests.

### Why It Matters

## Why It Matters

LVMH represents one of the most successful business models in the luxury industry. Rather than creating a single global brand, the Group has built its leadership by acquiring and developing independent Maisons while preserving each brand's identity, heritage, and creative direction. This unique balance between decentralization and operational scale has allowed LVMH to expand across multiple luxury sectors without compromising exclusivity.
Studying LVMH provides valuable insights into how long-term value is created in luxury through strategic acquisitions, portfolio diversification, brand management, and disciplined leadership. The company's ability to combine creativity, craftsmanship, financial performance, and global expansion makes it an exceptional case study for understanding modern luxury business.
For this reason, LVMH serves as an ideal starting point for this research project, offering a comprehensive perspective on the strategies, challenges, and opportunities that shape the global luxury industry today.
